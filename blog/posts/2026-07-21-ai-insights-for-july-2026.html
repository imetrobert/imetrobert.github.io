What This Means for Canadian Business

The "AI for All" strategy signals a major government push to integrate AI across the Canadian economy. The $200 million AI Missions Program for healthcare means that companies in this sector, such as those developing diagnostic tools, will see increased demand for AI-driven solutions. This focus on specific mission-driven projects can accelerate the deployment of new technologies, creating opportunities for Canadian innovators to partner with healthcare providers.

The Statistics Canada data showing 19.2% of Canadian businesses now use AI is a critical signal. While adoption has tripled since 2024, it remains uneven. Sectors like finance and insurance (40.4% adoption) and professional, scientific, and technical services (32.4% adoption) are leading the charge. This disparity highlights a growing gap between early adopters and the rest of the market, particularly in sectors like agriculture and manufacturing where adoption is significantly lower.

The introduction of Bill C-36, the Protecting Privacy and Consumer Data Act (PPCDA), directly addresses the need for modernized privacy laws. With a deadline for compliance looming, Canadian businesses must audit their data handling practices. This legislation, a cornerstone of the "AI for All" strategy, emphasizes privacy as a fundamental right and requires greater transparency in automated decision-making.

Strategic Actions for This Month

1
Audit current AI adoption and readiness against the "AI for All" strategy's goals. Identify gaps in literacy, infrastructure, and application. Assign the CTO and Head of Digital Transformation to lead this assessment by the end of Q3.
2
Pilot generative AI tools for internal process automation in at least two departments (e.g., HR, Finance) using the expanded Compute Access Fund resources. The CFO and relevant department heads will oversee this pilot, with results due by the end of Q3.
3
Review and update data privacy policies and consent mechanisms in light of Bill C-36 (PPCDA). Ensure compliance with new requirements for children's data and automated decision-making. The Legal team and Chief Privacy Officer must complete this review within 60 days.
4
Commission a study on the potential impact of AI on workforce skills within your specific industry, referencing the "AI for All" strategy's job creation projections. The CHRO will own this initiative, with findings due by the end of Q4.
5
Explore partnerships or investment opportunities related to the $500 million Canadian Tech Growth Fund, particularly if your company is seeking to scale AI capabilities. The CEO and Head of Corporate Development should investigate potential avenues before the end of the year.

Editor's Note
Robert's Take

“What I keep hearing from Canadian leaders right now is a mix of ambition and apprehension regarding the "AI for All" strategy. The government's commitment is clear, especially with the $2.3 billion investment and the focus on sovereign capabilities. However, the Statistics Canada data showing 19.2% AI adoption, while improved, still highlights a significant gap, particularly for SMEs. We need to move beyond just talking about AI and focus on practical implementation and demonstrable business outcomes, or we risk falling further behind.”

The Bottom Line

This legislation, a cornerstone of the "AI for All" strategy, emphasizes privacy as a fundamental right and requires greater transparency in automated decision-making. The organizations that act on this month's intelligence will set the AI standard in their sector for the next 12 months.
