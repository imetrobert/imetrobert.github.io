The Canadian government launched its "AI for All" strategy this month, a multi-billion-dollar plan aiming for massive economic growth and job creation. This signals a clear intent to position Canada as a global AI leader. This analysis cuts through the hype to show what it means for your business.

Key AI Developments This Month

June 05
Quartz

Reported that Canada launched a $500 million Canadian Tech Growth Fund to invest in domestic AI companies, potentially taking equity stakes. This fund aims to address the scale-up capital gap and keep Canadian talent and intellectual property within the country

🔍 Quartz
June 17
Miller Thomson

Analyzed the "AI for All" strategy, noting it does not introduce comprehensive AI legislation but outlines a framework for future regulations addressing privacy, online harms, and misinformation. The modernization of PIPEDA via Bill C-36 (PPCDA) is highlighted as a key upcoming development

🔍 Miller Thomson
June 22
IBM Institute for Business Value

Released findings indicating a "control gap" in Canadian organizations scaling AI, where governance, oversight, and workforce readiness lag behind adoption speed. 90% of Canadian CEOs report embedding AI across workflows, but only 43% have seen expected ROI

🔍 IBM Canada
June 24
Microsoft

Unveiled new AI-powered teaching and learning experiences at no additional cost, alongside its 2026 AI in Education Report. The report highlights that 92% of students and education leaders have used AI for school-related purposes, with 58% of leaders implementing or scaling AI

🔍 Microsoft

What's Happening in Canada

  • 🍁
    June 04
    Government of Canada — Launched "AI for All," a national AI strategy with six pillars: Trust, Empower Canadians, Shared Prosperity, Sovereign Foundation, Scale Champions, and Global Alliances. The strategy aims to create 250,000 new AI-related jobs and boost the economy by $200 billion over five years
    🔍 Government of Canada
  • 🍁
    June 12
    DLA Piper — Reported on the "AI for All" strategy, noting its focus on six pillars and five priority sectors: health/life sciences, energy/natural resources, transportation, agriculture, and manufacturing/robotics. The strategy also includes expanding the Sovereign Technology Alliance with Germany
    🔍 DLA Piper
  • 🍁
    June 15
    Government of Canada — Introduced Bill C-36, the Protecting Privacy and Consumer Data Act (PPCDA), to modernize Canada's private-sector privacy law. This bill aims to recognize privacy as a fundamental right and sets higher standards for handling children's information, with penalties up to $25 million or 5% of global revenue for serious offenses
    🔍 Government of Canada

What This Means for Canadian Business

The government's "AI for All" strategy signals a significant push for AI adoption and sovereign capabilities. This means Canadian financial institutions must accelerate their own AI integration to remain competitive. TD Bank's research shows Canadians are comfortable with AI for routine financial tasks but prefer human advisors for major decisions. This creates an imperative for banks to deploy AI for efficiency while ensuring human oversight for complex client needs.

In manufacturing and retail, the "AI for All" strategy highlights five priority sectors, including manufacturing. While specific Canadian company announcements are scarce this month, the broader trend indicates a need for these sectors to actively explore AI for productivity gains. The government's investment in the AI Compute Access Fund aims to lower barriers for SMEs, making compute power more attainable for adoption.

The introduction of Bill C-36 (PPCDA) and Bill C-34 (Safe Social Media Act) signals a coming wave of regulatory scrutiny. Canadian businesses must prepare for enhanced privacy obligations and specific rules for AI chatbot operators. The deadline for understanding and preparing for these changes is immediate, as these legislative proposals are already in motion.

Strategic Actions for This Month

1
Audit existing AI deployments for compliance with emerging privacy requirements outlined in Bill C-36 (PPCDA). Assign the Legal and Compliance teams to complete this by July 31, 2026.
2
Pilot generative AI tools for customer-facing administrative tasks, using Microsoft's new AI-powered teaching and learning experiences where applicable. Task the Digital Transformation Office to report on pilot outcomes within 60 days.
3
Review eligibility for the expanded AI Compute Access Fund, particularly for SMEs needing scalable compute power for AI development. Direct the CTO to submit applications for relevant projects by August 15, 2026.
4
Develop a clear policy for the responsible use of AI chatbots, informed by the proposed duties in Bill C-34. Assign the Product Development and Risk Management teams to draft this policy within 30 days.
5
Commission a cross-functional working group to assess the workforce readiness for AI adoption, focusing on upskilling and reskilling needs, in response to IBM's findings on the "control gap." The group must present initial recommendations by September 30, 2026.
